Coffee Lingo 101

Let's decode the language of the caf√©. Double-tap any term to see a visual example.

Coffee Grind

The coffee powder used for brewing.

Espresso / Shot / Base

A small amount of concentrated coffee used as the base for all coffee orders.

Crema

The thin, caramel-colored layer that forms on top of every shot of espresso. Also known as "coffee oil".

Tamper

The little black or stainless steel instrument used to press down the coffee in the porta-filter.

Group Handle (Coffee Gun)

The instrument that holds the porta-filter, held when putting coffee in the machine.

Group Head

The part of the machine the group handle fits into.

Element

A hot screen/plate inside the group head that filters the coffee grind and turns it into liquid.

Porta-Filter

A round silver cup that clips inside the group head.

Extraction

The process that turns coffee grind into coffee liquid.

Steam Wand

The long silver stem that comes out from the side of the machine, through which steam travels when heating milk.

Nozzle

The end tip on the steam wand with 3 or 4 holes where steam comes out.

Dissipates

To disappear or fade away (referring to crema that doesn't form properly).

The 3 Golden Secrets to Perfect Coffee

  1. 1. CREMA / COFFEE OIL - The essential layer that provides flavor and body
  2. 2. CREAMY, HOT MILK LIKE SILK - Perfectly textured milk for superior coffee drinks
  3. 3. MAKING WHAT THE CUSTOMER ORDERS - Whether weak, strong, boiled or lukewarm, making the customer happy keeps them coming back!

Crafting the Perfect Espresso Shot

1 Prep & Pre-heat: Always have appropriate cups (latte, cappuccino, long black) prepared and pre-heated with hot water before making the base/shot.

2 Dose: Take the group handle and fill the porta-filter with the measured amount of coffee grind: single shot (1 click), double shot (2 clicks).

3 Tamp: Press down the coffee using the tampa. DO NOT tamp too hard or tight, just enough till it's at required level so the coffee is balanced and evened out. Wipe away any excess grind.

4 Brew IMMEDIATELY: Place in machine head and press the required button immediately! The coffee WILL BURN if button is NOT pressed as soon as the coffee touches the hot element.

5 Extraction Time: The extraction should take between 25-30 seconds (varies by machine).

6 Grind Consistency: If shot takes 10-15 seconds, grind is too coarse. If 40-50 seconds, too fine. Espresso grind should have a "sand-like" texture.

7 Shot Volume: A shot of espresso should be 40-50ml (varies by grind/machine).

8 Check the Crema: KEY TO BEAUTIFUL COFFEE = THE CREMA. It should be 3-4ml thick with a smooth, caramel color.

Warning: If streaks or stripes appear of a darker orange color, the coffee could be bitter. Swirl the shot to blend dark stripes with caramel color. If crema dissipates, throw it away and make a fresh shot!

Pro Tip: For weak coffee, make the base as shown then pour half out carefully without losing crema. For strong coffee, use a double shot but stop manually before programmed time.

Mastering Milk Textures

Watch this demonstration of proper milk texturing techniques

CREAMY MILK ...LIKE SILK (Recommended)

1 Pour fresh, cold milk (4¬∞C) into the jug.

2 Place the nozzle of the steam wand in the milk to the bottom and turn steam on.

3 Lower the jug until the nozzle is just below the surface = KISSING THE MILK.

4 Keep nozzle just below surface AT ALL TIMES, moving the wand around slightly.

5 As milk heats and rises, continue lowering the jug and adjust pressure to prevent boiling.

Result: Creamy, silky, thick texture perfect for lattes, cappuccinos, flat whites, etc.

STEWED / STEAMED MILK (Not Recommended)

Nozzle placed at bottom of jug with high pressure, swirling rapidly. Results in thin, hot milk that changes coffee flavor.

FLUFFY / FROTHY MILK (Not Recommended)

Nozzle brought to surface and out of milk to create big air bubbles. Results in lukewarm, thin milk with fluffy froth on top.

Important: NEVER heat milk twice. NEVER use burnt milk. ALWAYS use fresh milk. Only use leftover milk for "hot milk on the side" requests.

How to Make Latte Art

Learn the techniques for creating beautiful latte art designs

Basic Latte Art Techniques

1 Perfect Milk Texture: Start with properly textured milk - silky and glossy with microfoam.

2 Proper Pouring Height: Begin pouring from a higher position to sink the milk beneath the crema.

3 Lower for Design: Bring the pitcher close to the surface when you're ready to create your design.

4 Control Flow: Maintain a steady, controlled pour to create consistent patterns.

5 Finish Clean: Lift the pitcher at the end and pull through to finish your design.

Pro Tip: Practice with water and a drop of dish soap first to get the pouring motion right without wasting milk!

Cup: Ceramic, pre-heated

Flat White

Flat White

Cup: Ceramic, pre-heated

Shots: 1 shot

Milk: Hot and creamy (70-80¬∞C)

Method: Pour without disturbing crema, slowly raise jug high, then bring low when halfway.

Finish: Stop 1cm before rim, allow foam to form, then fill to top. Leave white circle for design.

Espresso

Espresso / Short Black

Cup: Espresso cup, pre-heated

Shots: 1 shot

Method: Tip water out from side customer won't drink from (usually back if handle on right).

Extraction: Ensure coffee doesn't splash to preserve crema.

Long Black

Long Espresso / Long Black / Americano / Regular

Cup: Ceramic, pre-heated

Shots: DOUBLE (2 shots)

Method: Tip water out, refill to 60-70% with hot water, begin extraction INTO THE TOP of hot water.

Important: Coffee must go in on top of water for smoother flavor and crema formation. Reverse order burns coffee and destroys crema.

Macciato

Short Macciato

Meaning: "Stain" in Italian - strong black coffee with a dash of milk

Method: Follow espresso procedure, then add 2 teaspoons of thin milk and a dollop of creamy foam in middle of crema.

Long Macciato: Follow Long Espresso procedure, add 2 tablespoons of thin milk and spoon of creamy foam.

Ristretto

Ristretto

Meaning: "Restriction" in Italian - half of a short espresso

Method: Follow espresso procedure but stop shot when it reaches halfway.

Character: Extra strong shot using the most potent first drops of extraction.

Piccolo

Piccolo

Description: A mini-latte for an extra strong hit

Method: Follow Ristretto procedure, then pour hot, creamy milk into espresso cup bringing crema to top.

Finish: Leave white circle in middle for design presentation.

Mocha

Mocha

Glass: Latte glass, pre-heated

Ingredients: 1 heaped teaspoon chocolate powder + 1 shot espresso

Milk: Creamed until hot (like latte)

Method: Pour milk bringing crema up, stop at 70-75%, stir in chocolate while waiting for separation.

Finish: Continue pouring, foam above rim. Create thick chocolate circle on foam.

Vienna

Vienna

Glass: Latte glass, pre-heated

Shots: DOUBLE (2 shots)

Method: Extract double shot into glass, fill with hot water to 70-75% full.

Finish: Add 2¬Ω swirls of whipped cream on top, light dusting of chocolate powder.

Affogato

Affogato

Vessel: Latte glass or coffee drinking bowl

Ingredients: 2 small scoops or 1 large scoop of ice cream + DOUBLE (2 shots) espresso

Method: Place ice cream in glass/bowl, extract espresso straight over ice cream.

Finish: Light dusting of chocolate powder.

Hot Chocolate

Hot Chocolate

Glass: Latte glass, pre-heated

Ingredients: 1-2 heaped teaspoons chocolate powder + dash of chocolate sauce (if available)

Milk: Extra hot (at least 80¬∞C) as chocolate powder cools it down

Method: Pour milk to 70-75%, stir in chocolate, continue pouring.

Finish: Like mocha or cappuccino, create perfect chocolate circle on foam.

Important Coffee Facts

The First Drops Are the Strongest: The most potent part of a coffee shot is the first drops/drips that come out (espresso, ristretto, piccolo, short macciato). These are considered "fixes" or a "hit" - the most intense, strongest ways of drinking coffee.

Dilution Softens Coffee: When coffee is left to run longer past 40-50ml, or has milk/water added, it becomes diluted, taking away intensity but softening the flavor.

No Crema = No Flavor: Crema is essential for body and flavor. Streaks/stripes in crema indicate burnt/bitter coffee.
